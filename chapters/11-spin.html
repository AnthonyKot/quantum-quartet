Chapter XI · Part III — The Physics

Spin

Spin is the property with no classical picture. An electron carries angular momentum that is not the momentum of anything going around — it does not shrink as you imagine the particle shrinking, it does not vanish for a point, and it takes half-integer values that no rotating object can. It is the cleanest example in the whole subject of a quantity that exists only in the quantum theory, and for that reason it is where the four authors' methods separate most sharply. This is the widest stylistic spread in the project, and the chapter is built to let the contrast do the work.

At one pole stands Feynman, who introduces spin through the Stern-Gerlach experiment and refuses any classical analogy from the first sentence. At the other stands Dirac, who never mentions a beam of atoms and derives the spin matrices from algebra alone. Between them, Landau and Lifshitz reach spin from the symmetry of space — the behavior of the wave function under rotations — while Born grounds it in the experimental anomalies, the doublets and Zeeman patterns, that forced physicists to grant the electron an intrinsic spin in the first place.

The four treatments

Feynman: spin before "angular momentum"

Feynman makes spin the entry point to quantum mechanics itself. He opens the relevant chapter by declaring that he will describe a quantum phenomenon in a fully quantum way, making no apology and no attempt to connect it to classical mechanics — announcing outright that he will not lean on classical concepts like "angular momentum" until later, so that nothing classical smuggles itself in. (He names the phrase in his first sentence, to set it aside; the discipline is in the reasoning that follows, which appeals only to the experiment.) The phenomenon is the Stern-Gerlach experiment: a beam of atoms passes through an inhomogeneous magnetic field and splits into a fixed number of beams, three for what he calls a particle of spin one. Block two beams, and the survivor passing through a second identical apparatus is not re-split — it has been filtered into a definite state.

From this apparatus, used as a filter, Feynman builds the entire machinery of states, base states, and transformation amplitudes. In the spin one-half chapter he goes further, deriving the coefficients that relate spin measured along one axis to spin measured along another "by pure reasoning" from a few assumptions about the structure of space — an argument he cheerfully calls a "cultural excursion." The point is that spin, the least classical of quantities, is for Feynman the ideal teaching example precisely because it forces the reader to think in amplitudes from the start, with no classical crutch to lean on. Spin is not a complication added late; it is the doorway.

Dirac: the matrices from the algebra

Dirac reaches the same spin-one-half physics without a single beam of atoms. He posits spin variables $\sigma$ obeying a short list of algebraic relations — each component squares to one, and different components anticommute — and then asks what matrices can represent them. The answer is forced. Taking $\sigma_z$ diagonal with eigenvalues $\pm 1$, the requirement that $\sigma_x$ be Hermitian, square to one, and anticommute with $\sigma_z$ leaves only the off-diagonal form; fixing the remaining phase gives the three Pauli matrices outright. The two-valuedness of the electron, the whole structure of a spin-½ system, drops out of the algebra with no physical input beyond the defining relations.

This is Dirac's method in its purest form. A state of the spin is a pair of numbers — the representative of a ket over the two-point domain of $\sigma_z$ — and a general spin direction is a superposition of the up and down states with weights read straight off the matrix. Where Feynman earns every amplitude from an experiment, Dirac earns them from consistency conditions, and arrives at identical matrices. Spin, which began as the most stubbornly physical of discoveries, becomes for Dirac an exercise in finding the smallest matrices that satisfy an algebra.

Landau & Lifshitz: spin as a fact about rotations

Landau and Lifshitz derive spin from the symmetry of space. They observe first that the quantum formalism permits a particle to carry an internal discrete variable beyond its coordinates, and that there is no reason to assume an elementary particle lacks one. This intrinsic angular momentum, they stress, is "peculiar to quantum theory" — it disappears in the limit $\hbar \to 0$ and is therefore incapable of any classical interpretation; imagining it as the particle spinning on "its own axis" is, in their word, meaningless.

The technical core is that the spin operators must satisfy the same commutation relations as orbital angular momentum, but for a deeper reason: those relations express how the system behaves under infinitesimal rotations of space, and that holds whether the angular momentum lives in the coordinates or in an internal variable. Crucially, the derivation that forces orbital angular momentum to be integer-valued relies on the coordinate representation and fails for spin — so half-integer values become allowed, and $\mathbf{s}^2 = s(s+1)$ with $s$ integer or half-integer. Their §55 draws the consequence that most captures the strangeness: under a full $2\pi$ rotation of the coordinate system, an integer-spin wave function returns to itself, but a half-integer-spin one changes sign. Spin, for Landau and Lifshitz, is what the rotation group demands once you allow the wave function more than one component.

Born: the anomalies that forced the spin

Born comes at spin the way the physics actually arrived: through spectroscopic facts that classical and old-quantum ideas could not explain. His chapter on the subject opens with alkali doublets — the splitting of spectral lines in sodium and its cousins into close pairs — and the anomalous Zeeman effect, in which a magnetic field splits lines into more components than Bohr's theory allows. These anomalies, Born explains, cannot be understood without granting the electron an intrinsic mechanical angular momentum and magnetic moment of its own, the proposal Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit made in 1925.

Born's treatment keeps the phenomenon in the foreground and introduces the formalism — including the Pauli spin theory and its matrices — only as the tool the phenomenon requires. He is interested in the doublets and the Zeeman patterns as measured facts, and in showing that the spinning electron accounts for them quantitatively. Where Feynman uses an idealized apparatus to introduce spin cleanly and Dirac uses none at all, Born uses the messy real spectra that historically made the concept unavoidable. His entry point is the evidence, not the idealization.

Synthesis

Spin lays the four temperaments side by side more starkly than any other topic. Feynman stages a clean experiment and refuses all classical language, making spin the purest demonstration of amplitude thinking. Dirac stages no experiment at all and lets an algebra force the matrices into existence. Landau and Lifshitz derive spin from the rotation group, treating it as a structural necessity of any multi-component wave function. Born tracks the historical evidence — the doublets, the anomalous Zeeman splittings — that compelled physicists to accept spin before any of these formalisms were in place. Idealized experiment, pure algebra, symmetry, and evidence: four doors into one room.

The Feynman–Dirac contrast is the sharpest in the project, and spin is where it is most visible because both reach the same object — the two-component spin-½ state and its Pauli matrices — from opposite starting points. Feynman will not write down a matrix until an experiment has earned it; Dirac will not appeal to an experiment when consistency conditions suffice. A reader who learns spin from Feynman comes away feeling she has watched atoms sort themselves in a magnet; a reader who learns it from Dirac comes away feeling she has solved a small algebra problem. Both have the identical physics, and neither picture is more correct — which is the whole thesis of this project, sharpened to its finest point.

What none of them will allow is the picture the word "spin" invites: a little ball rotating. Feynman announces he will set classical concepts aside and reason only from the experiment; Landau and Lifshitz call the spinning-ball image meaningless outright; Dirac never offers a picture to be tempted by; Born treats the electron's angular momentum as a measured property, not a mechanical rotation. The half-integer values and the $2\pi$ sign change are the theory's way of insisting that spin is genuinely new. On that the four authors, for all their differences of method, do not disagree.
