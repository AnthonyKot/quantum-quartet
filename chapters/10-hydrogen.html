The Hydrogen Atom

Hydrogen is the test every quantum theory must pass. One electron bound to one proton by the Coulomb force is the simplest atom, and it is the only atom that can be solved exactly. Its spectrum — the sharp lines Balmer fit to a formula in 1885, decades before anyone could explain them — is the anvil on which the new mechanics was hammered out. When the Schrödinger equation reproduced the Balmer series from a law of motion, quantum mechanics stopped being a promising scheme and became the theory of the atom.

The four authors converge on the same energy levels, $E_n = -\tfrac{m\alpha^2}{2\hbar^2 n^2}$, by four routes that reveal their temperaments completely. Landau solves the Coulomb problem as a set-piece of mathematical technique. Born tells the story of why the answer mattered before anyone could derive it. Dirac reaches the same radial equation through the abstract algebra of a central field, descending to coordinates only to finish. Feynman, near the end of Volume III, does the one thing he has avoided for eighteen chapters: he sits down and grinds through the differential equation.

The four treatments

Landau & Lifshitz: the Coulomb problem as set-piece

For Landau and Lifshitz the hydrogen atom is the culmination of their chapter on centrally symmetric fields, and they solve it with characteristic economy. They switch to "Coulomb units" — the atomic units in which $m$, $\hbar$, and the Coulomb constant are all set to one — so the radial equation loses its clutter of constants. Substituting the expected asymptotic behavior reduces the equation to the confluent hypergeometric function, and the physical requirement that the solution not blow up at infinity forces the series to terminate. That termination is the quantization: the principal quantum number $n$ must be a positive integer with $n \ge l+1$, and the energy $E = -1/2n^2$ falls out immediately.

Two features mark the treatment as theirs. First, they dwell on the accidental degeneracy — the fact that the energy depends only on $n$ and not on the angular momentum $l$, giving each level a degeneracy of $n^2$. In a general central field the energy would depend on both; that it does not is a special property of the $1/r$ potential, and they flag it as such rather than letting it pass unremarked. Second, they do not stop at the bound states. They push through the entire continuous spectrum of positive-energy Coulomb wave functions, representing them by contour integrals and extracting the scattering asymptotics — a completeness no other author here attempts. For Landau the hydrogen atom is not just the answer to a physical question; it is an exhibition of how far exact methods reach.

Born: the answer before the derivation

Born's hydrogen chapter is history first and calculation second, and deliberately so. He begins with the crisis: a classical electron orbiting a proton radiates continuously, spirals inward, and the atom collapses in a fraction of a second. That hydrogen exists at all, and emits not a smear of frequencies but a discrete series of razor-sharp lines, is something classical physics "cannot understand" — his framing of the impasse that forced the quantum. He then gives Balmer his due: the 1885 formula that fit the visible hydrogen lines to a difference of two terms, accurate to the last decimal the spectroscopists could measure, long before anyone knew what the terms were.

The decisive step, in Born's telling, is recognizing that the form of Balmer's formula — a difference of two expressions of the same type — points straight at energy levels, once Bohr's condition ties a spectral line to a jump between them. The Rydberg constant, measured to six figures, becomes a combination of $e$, $m$, and $\hbar$, and the correspondence principle fixes how. Born is leading the chapter's meaning: he supplies the reason the eventual solution is a triumph, which is that it recovers, from a bare equation of motion, a number the experimentalists had known to extraordinary precision for forty years.

Dirac: hydrogen as an application of the algebra

Dirac reaches hydrogen through the most abstract door. Before writing any wave equation he sets up the central-field problem in the language of observables: he defines a radial momentum $p_r$ satisfying the canonical relation $[r, p_r] = i\hbar$, expresses the angular momentum magnitude $k$ algebraically, and manipulates Poisson brackets until the Hamiltonian appears in the reduced form $H = \frac{1}{2m}\!\left(\frac{1}{r}p_r^2\,r + \frac{k(k+\hbar)}{r^2}\right) + V$. All of this happens in the abstract theory, without committing to a representation, and it works for any central potential $V$.

Only then does he descend. Introducing Schrödinger's representation with coordinates diagonal turns the abstract equation into a differential equation in $r$, $\theta$, $\phi$; the angular part is the spherical-harmonic operator whose eigenvalues are $l(l+1)\hbar^2$ (Dirac writes the order as $n$, which is not the principal quantum number used elsewhere in this chapter), and the radial part becomes the equation to solve for the Coulomb $V = -\alpha/r$. Dirac even pauses on a subtlety the others skip — that the boundary condition at the origin must be checked against the full Cartesian equation, since $\nabla^2(1/r)$ hides a delta function. His hydrogen atom is a worked example of a general machine, and the machine is the point.

Feynman: grinding the equation at last

Feynman's hydrogen chapter is startling precisely because of what it is not. Having spent seventeen chapters insisting that quantum mechanics is best done with amplitudes and two-state systems, avoiding differential equations almost entirely, he arrives at hydrogen and simply solves the Schrödinger equation the old-fashioned way. He writes the spherically symmetric equation, admits that there is no general method for such equations and that "you just have to fiddle around," proposes a series solution, and finds that physically acceptable solutions — ones that vanish at large $r$ — exist only for the quantized energies. The same brute-force method, extended to non-zero angular momentum, gives the full set of states labeled by three quantum numbers.

What makes the chapter Feynman's, despite the conventional method, is the frame he puts around it. He is explicit that the experimental hydrogen spectrum was known in advance, and that reproducing it from "a basic equation of motion for the electron" was "the first great success of the Schrödinger theory." The calculation is standard; the emphasis is on what it meant. And the placement is itself a statement: hydrogen is where his amplitude-first pedagogy finally cashes out into the concrete spatial problem it had deferred, arriving at the same Balmer numbers by the route he had spent the book postponing.

Synthesis

Hydrogen is the chapter where all four authors must produce the same number, and the interest lies entirely in how they get there. Landau treats the Coulomb potential as an opportunity to display exact technique, solving even the continuous spectrum that the physics does not strictly require. Dirac treats it as an application of his central-field algebra, deriving the radial problem abstractly and descending to coordinates only to finish. Feynman treats it as the overdue differential-equation calculation, done plainly and framed by its historical weight. Born treats it as a story whose climax the others are computing — the forty-year gap between Balmer's formula and its explanation.

The sharpest contrast is between Dirac and Feynman, and it inverts their usual roles. Through most of the book Feynman is the physical, intuitive one and Dirac the abstract formalist. Here Dirac derives the Hamiltonian from Poisson brackets in pure operator algebra, while Feynman rolls up his sleeves and solves a differential equation by trial series — the concrete grind Dirac's abstraction is built to avoid. The Coulomb problem is where Feynman pays the bill his amplitude pedagogy had run up, and where Dirac shows that his abstraction was never ornamental: it reduces the atom to a one-dimensional radial equation before a single coordinate is chosen.

Underneath the four methods sits one fact that none of them invented and all of them recover: the energies scale as $-1/n^2$, and hydrogen's peculiar $l$-degeneracy is a gift of the Coulomb force alone. Born knew the number from Balmer; Bohr guessed the levels; and the new mechanics, in every one of these four dialects, derives them. That convergence — a spectroscopic constant known to six figures, falling out of an equation of motion — is the moment the theory earned its authority, and each author, in his own register, is describing the same victory.
