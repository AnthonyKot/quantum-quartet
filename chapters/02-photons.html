Photons: Photoelectric Effect and Compton Scattering

If Planck's quanta were a reluctant concession, Einstein's photons were an act of intellectual courage. In 1905 — the same year he published special relativity — Einstein proposed that light itself consists of discrete packets of energy, each carrying energy $E = h\nu$ and momentum $p = h\nu/c$. This was not a reinterpretation of Planck's formula; it was a claim about the nature of light itself.

The photoelectric effect provided the decisive evidence. When light shines on a metal surface, electrons are ejected — but only if the light's frequency exceeds a certain threshold, regardless of the light's intensity. Einstein's explanation was simple: each electron absorbs a single photon, and the photon's energy must exceed the binding energy of the electron. Increasing the intensity of the light increases the number of electrons ejected but not their energy; increasing the frequency increases the energy of each electron.

The Compton effect (1923) sealed the case. Arthur Compton showed that X-rays scattered from electrons undergo a wavelength shift that can be calculated by treating the scattering as a collision between a photon and an electron — a genuine billiard-ball collision, with conservation of energy and momentum applied to quantized packets of light.

Born's historical treatment

Born devotes substantial space to the photoelectric effect and the Compton effect in Atomic Physics. He treats them as the empirical foundation of the photon concept — the experiments that forced physicists to abandon the wave theory of light. Born's account is careful and patient: he explains the experimental setup, the puzzling observations, and Einstein's explanation in turn.

Born is notable for not rushing to embrace the photon concept. He recognizes that the wave theory of light had been spectacularly successful, and he does not want to discard it lightly. His treatment reflects the caution of a physicist who has seen too many premature theories to accept a radical new idea without solid evidence.

Feynman's counterpoint

Feynman's treatment of the photon is split across the Lectures in a way worth noticing. The photoelectric effect and Compton scattering — the historical evidence for light quanta — belong to Volume I, not to the quantum-mechanics Volume III. By the time Volume III opens, Feynman takes the photon for granted and moves straight to interference and amplitudes. He emphasizes the tension between the particle and wave pictures of light, framing the photon not as a tiny ball of light but as a quantum of excitation of the electromagnetic field.

Feynman's approach is more modern than Born's. He does not present the photon as a replacement for the wave but as a complement — a different aspect of the same underlying reality. This is the perspective of quantum electrodynamics, which was still being developed at the time Feynman was writing, but which he understood deeply.

Landau & Lifshitz: deferral by design

In the Course of Theoretical Physics, the photon is not a subject for the quantum-mechanics volume at all. That volume is explicitly non-relativistic, and the photon — a massless, intrinsically relativistic quantum of the electromagnetic field — is deferred to the volume on quantum electrodynamics. Where Born opens with light quanta as experimental fact, Landau and Lifshitz simply route the question elsewhere.

This deferral is not an oversight but a statement about architecture. The Course is built so that each volume rests on a clean set of assumptions; the photon does not fit the non-relativistic ones, so it waits. The reader who wants Landau and Lifshitz on photons is pointed, in effect, to a different book.

Dirac and the quantized field

Dirac's treatment of the photon in the Principles of Quantum Mechanics goes beyond the particle picture. He derives the photon concept from the quantization of the electromagnetic field — the same approach that Feynman would later use in quantum electrodynamics.

This is the deepest treatment of the four. Dirac does not treat the photon as a particle that happens to have wave-like properties; he treats it as a quantum of excitation of a field, which is the modern definition. But this depth comes at a cost: Dirac's treatment assumes a level of mathematical sophistication that the other three authors do not require.

Synthesis

The chapter on photons reveals something subtle about the four authors' relationship to the history of quantum mechanics. Born is a historian first and a physicist second: he tells the story of how the photon concept emerged, with all its uncertainty and resistance. Feynman is a teacher first: he presents the photon as a mystery to be contemplated, not a puzzle to be solved. Landau and Lifshitz are builders: they take the photon as a given and use it to construct the theory. Dirac is a logician: he derives the photon from first principles and shows that it was inevitable.

The tension between the particle and wave pictures of light is the central mystery of quantum mechanics, and the photon is its most immediate manifestation. Einstein's 1905 paper did not solve this tension; it made it sharper. The wave picture was too successful to abandon, but the particle picture was too useful to dismiss.

The resolution — quantum field theory — was not available to any of these authors in the form we know it today. Dirac began the path toward it, but the full theory would emerge only in the 1940s and 1950s. Until then, the photon remained a paradox: a particle that interferes with itself, a wave that is counted by individual clicks.
