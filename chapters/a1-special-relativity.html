Appendix A · Relativity

Spacetime and Lorentz Transformations

The appendix steps outside the quantum quartet. Relativity is the other pillar of twentieth-century physics, and the four quantum authors mostly assume it rather than build it — so here the lead passes to the person who did build it. Einstein's own 1916 popular exposition, written for the general reader, is the primary source for these chapters, with Feynman's Volume I as a modern companion. The comparison is narrower than in the main chapters, but the contrast between how the discoverer and a later teacher present the same ideas is worth drawing.

Special relativity rests on two premises that seem, at first, to contradict each other: the laws of physics are the same in every uniformly moving frame (the principle of relativity), and light travels at the same speed $c$ regardless of the motion of its source or observer. Reconciling them forces a revision of the most basic notions — simultaneity, length, and duration cease to be absolute — and the mathematics of that revision is the Lorentz transformation. Einstein reaches it by thinking about trains and lightning; Feynman reaches it through Maxwell's equations and the Michelson–Morley experiment.

The two treatments

Einstein: from simultaneity outward

Einstein's exposition is a masterpiece of building difficulty slowly. He begins with the innocuous-seeming law that light travels at a fixed speed $c$, and asks what happens if a ray is chased by a railway carriage moving at speed $v$. Common sense — the classical addition of velocities — says the carriage should measure the light approaching at $c - v$. But the principle of relativity insists that the law of light propagation must look the same from the carriage as from the embankment, and those two demands cannot both hold if velocities simply add. Something has to give.

What gives, Einstein shows, is simultaneity itself. Before touching any equation he dismantles the idea that "at the same time" has an observer-independent meaning. Two lightning strokes hit the embankment at points $A$ and $B$; an observer at the midpoint who sees the flashes together calls them simultaneous. But an observer on a moving train, passing the midpoint as the light travels, meets the flash from the front before the flash from behind — and so, by the same definition, judges them not simultaneous. Simultaneity is relative to the frame. From this single realization the whole structure follows: if clocks in different places cannot be synchronized absolutely, then time, length, and the addition of velocities must all be reworked, and the reworking is the Lorentz transformation. Einstein's genius in the popular book is to make the strange conclusion feel forced rather than imposed — the reader arrives at it having refused, at each step, to accept anything that cannot be checked by experiment.

He is candid that the resolution came from experience, not pure thought. It was Lorentz's investigation of the electrodynamics of moving bodies, he notes, that showed the constancy of light to be a necessary consequence of electromagnetic theory — and the theory that reconciled it with relativity he calls the special theory, to distinguish it from the general theory to come. The Minkowski four-dimensional world, in which space and time merge into a single continuum, he presents as the geometrical culmination: not a new physical assumption but the natural language in which the Lorentz transformation becomes a rotation.

Feynman: from Maxwell's symmetry

Feynman comes at special relativity from the direction that historically produced it: the failure of Maxwell's equations to respect the old, common-sense (Galilean) transformation between moving frames. He recounts the puzzle plainly. The principle of relativity had served mechanics since Newton, but Maxwell's electrodynamics seemed to violate it — the equations changed form under a Galilean transformation, which would let an experimenter detect absolute motion by optical means. The Michelson–Morley experiment of 1887 looked for exactly that absolute motion through the supposed ether, and found nothing, and the null result waited eighteen years for Einstein's explanation.

The resolution Feynman emphasizes is Lorentz's discovery that Maxwell's equations do keep their form under a different substitution — the Lorentz transformation — and Einstein's bold generalization that all physical laws must share that invariance. The consequence is that it is Newton's mechanics, not Maxwell's electrodynamics, that must be changed: mass has to be replaced by the velocity-dependent form $m = m_0/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$ for the two theories to harmonize. Feynman's framing is that of a modern physicist looking back with the answer in hand — he leads with the symmetry principle (invariance under the Lorentz transformation) and shows the mechanics bending to fit it, where Einstein led with the analysis of time and let the transformation emerge. Same destination, opposite order: Einstein reasons his way to the transformation from the meaning of simultaneity; Feynman starts from the transformation as the known symmetry of electrodynamics and asks what it costs the rest of physics.

Synthesis

The two accounts are a study in how a revolutionary idea looks from inside and from after. Einstein, writing eleven years after the discovery but still close to it, builds the theory from a philosophical analysis of simultaneity, taking pains to admit nothing the reader cannot in principle verify — the constancy of light is a fact about the world, and everything else is forced by refusing to smuggle in an absolute time. Feynman, writing half a century later, builds it from the symmetry of Maxwell's equations, treating Lorentz invariance as a principle to be imposed on all of physics and following its consequences. The discoverer argues upward from an experiment; the teacher argues downward from a symmetry.

What both refuse is the ether and the absolute frame it implied. Einstein dismantles absolute simultaneity by thought; Feynman lets Michelson and Morley dismantle absolute motion by measurement; and both arrive at the same Lorentz transformation and the same Minkowski spacetime, in which the interval between events, not their separation in space or time alone, is the invariant. That a popular exposition by the theory's author and a lecture course by its most famous expositor should converge so exactly — differing only in whether simultaneity or symmetry comes first — is a reminder that special relativity, unlike the quantum theory that fills the rest of this site, is a settled subject with a single agreed shape. The interpretive disagreements that animate the quantum chapters have no counterpart here. There is one relativity, told two ways.
