Blackbody Radiation and Planck's Quantum

The story of quantum mechanics begins not with a revolution but with a calculation that no one believed in. Max Planck wanted to derive a formula for the spectrum of blackbody radiation — the light emitted by a cavity in thermal equilibrium — and he found one. But the trick that made it work was absurd: he assumed that energy could only be exchanged in discrete chunks, $\Delta E = h\nu$, where $h$ is a new fundamental constant and $\nu$ is the frequency of the radiation.

Planck did not believe his own trick. He spent years trying to derive it from classical principles, convinced that the quantization was a mathematical convenience rather than a feature of nature. It took ten years for anyone to take it seriously, and when Einstein did — in 1905, in his paper on the photoelectric effect — he went further than Planck ever dared, proposing that light itself consists of quanta, not just that the exchange of energy between matter and radiation is quantized.

This chapter is the origin story. It is also the chapter where three of our four authors are conspicuously absent: Feynman's Vol. III, Landau and Lifshitz, and Dirac barely touch blackbody radiation, each for a different reason. Their near-absence is not a gap to be papered over but a cell to be filled with silence.

The Rayleigh–Jeans formula, derived from classical equipartition, predicts that the radiated power diverges at short wavelengths — the "ultraviolet catastrophe" that Planck's formula cured. Born treats this not at the front of Atomic Physics but well into it, in the chapter on quantum statistics, where heat radiation and Planck's law sit alongside specific heats and the Bose–Einstein and Fermi–Dirac distributions. That placement is itself a choice: for Born, blackbody radiation is not the dramatic opening of the quantum story but one thread in the statistical mechanics of quanta.

The crisis

The problem was deceptively simple. A blackbody is an idealized object that absorbs and emits radiation perfectly — a cavity with a small hole, where radiation entering the hole is trapped by multiple reflections. Inside such a cavity, at thermal equilibrium at temperature $T$, the radiation has a characteristic spectrum: the intensity of emitted light at each wavelength is determined solely by the temperature, not by the material of the cavity.

Classical physics predicted the spectrum using the equipartition theorem: each mode of the electromagnetic field in the cavity should carry an average energy of $k_B T$, where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant. Since the number of modes increases with the square of the frequency, the total radiated power at high frequencies grows without bound. This is the ultraviolet catastrophe.

Planck's solution was to assume that the oscillators in the cavity walls could only exchange energy with the field in discrete amounts: $E = nh\nu$, where $n$ is an integer. The resulting formula agreed with experiment at all wavelengths and reduced to Wien's law at high frequencies and to Rayleigh–Jeans at low ones. It was a triumph — but it came at a cost that nobody understood at the time.

The absence of Feynman, Landau & Lifshitz, and Dirac

Why do Feynman, Landau and Lifshitz, and Dirac barely mention blackbody radiation? The answer lies in their starting points.

Feynman begins the Feynman Lectures with the double-slit experiment — the phenomenon that, in his view, encapsulates the essence of quantum mechanics. Blackbody radiation is a warm-up act, not the main event. Feynman treats it briefly as historical context for the photon concept, but he does not dwell on the crisis or Planck's derivation.

Landau and Lifshitz begin with the uncertainty principle and the measurement problem. Blackbody radiation is not a good starting point for their approach: it is a statistical phenomenon involving many degrees of freedom, not a single-particle puzzle. They treat it in the context of statistical physics (Volume V of the series), not in the quantum mechanics volume.

Dirac's approach is the most austere. He begins with the logical structure of quantum mechanics — states, observables, and the transformation theory that unifies matrices and wave functions. Blackbody radiation is an application, not a foundation, and Dirac does not waste time on historical derivations.

This triply repeated absence is itself a data point. Three of the four books treat blackbody radiation as a historical curiosity — a step on the path to the real theory, not the theory itself. Born, the only one who treats it seriously, is the one author who was actually working on it at the time.

Synthesis

The lesson of this chapter is that the birth of quantum mechanics was not a dramatic confrontation with experiment but a slow, reluctant accommodation. Planck did not wake up one day and decide that energy was quantized. He tried classical methods, failed, and stumbled onto a formula that worked — then spent years trying to justify it classically.

This is the first of several moments in the history of quantum mechanics where a successful formula preceded its explanation by years or decades. The Bohr model of the atom (1913) anticipated wave mechanics by twelve years. Matrix mechanics (1925) and wave mechanics (1926) arrived independently and were shown to be equivalent only later.

Born's treatment of this history is valuable precisely because he was there. He witnessed the debate between Planck and Einstein over the physical meaning of quanta. He knew Heisenberg personally and helped him develop matrix mechanics. His account is not a neutral history but the testimony of a participant — and that gives it a texture that later retellings lack.
