Chapter VI · Part II — The New Mechanics

The Wavefunction and Schrödinger's Equation

The wavefunction $\psi$ is the central object of non-relativistic quantum mechanics, and its equation of motion — the Schrödinger equation — is the workhorse that every practical calculation eventually runs on. But where does the equation come from? It cannot be deduced from anything more fundamental; it is a law of nature, tested by its consequences. The four authors differ sharply in how they handle that fact — whether they motivate the equation by symmetry, postulate it outright, present it as the integrated form of a deeper abstraction, or frankly admit it was guessed.

This is the chapter where Landau's register is at full strength and where Feynman's ordering becomes a subject in itself. Landau and Lifshitz reach the Schrödinger equation early and derive as much of its form as symmetry allows. Feynman reaches it in his Chapter 16, sixteen chapters into Volume III, and says so pointedly. Born supplies the reading of $\psi$ that makes the equation about physics rather than mathematics. Dirac has already written the equation down, in the previous chapter's terms, as one face of his abstract law of motion.

The four treatments

Landau & Lifshitz: as much as symmetry allows

Landau and Lifshitz do the thing that most distinguishes their book: they refuse to simply write the Schrödinger equation down. Instead they ask what the Hamiltonian of a free particle can be, given the symmetry of space. For a free particle the Hamiltonian cannot depend on position — space is homogeneous — so it must be built from momentum alone; and because space is isotropic, it can depend only on the magnitude of the momentum. Galilean relativity then forces the quadratic dependence $E = p^2/2m$, and promoting this to operators gives the free-particle Hamiltonian $\hat H = -(\hbar^2/2m)\Delta$ almost without choice. Adding a potential $U$ for an interacting system yields the full equation $i\hbar\,\partial_t\Psi = -(\hbar^2/2m)\Delta\Psi + U\Psi$.

What symmetry cannot fix — the interaction term — they take over from classical mechanics by the requirement that the theory reduce correctly in the classical limit. And they show that limit explicitly: substituting $\Psi = a\,e^{iS/\hbar}$ and letting $\hbar \to 0$ recovers the Hamilton–Jacobi equation for the classical action $S$, while the modulus $|a|^2 = |\Psi|^2$ obeys a continuity equation, flowing with the classical velocity. The Schrödinger equation is not handed down; it is cornered from two directions — symmetry above, classical mechanics below — until only one form is left. This is the calculational register operating on the foundations themselves.

Feynman: the equation that came out of a mind

Feynman's treatment is defined by when it happens. He arrives at the Schrödinger equation only in Chapter 16 of Volume III, and he opens that chapter by announcing that he is breaking his usual rule — he will write down the differential equation for amplitudes in space without having time to develop its consequences. The placement is the message: everything essential about quantum behavior, in his telling, was already available from amplitudes and the two-slit experiment. The Schrödinger equation is a late specialization, not a foundation.

His route to it is physical rather than deductive. He imagines an electron hopping along a line of atoms in a crystal, writes the amplitude to be at each site, and takes the continuum limit; the resulting equation has the form of a diffusion equation — but with an imaginary coefficient in front of the time derivative, which turns spreading into wave propagation. Then he is scrupulously honest about status. He tells the reader outright that the equation is not derived from anything: in his framing it came out of Schrödinger's mind, from heuristic arguments and intuitive guesses, some of them even wrong, and the only thing that matters is that the final equation describes nature correctly. Where Landau corners the equation with symmetry, Feynman motivates it with a picture and then confesses that motivation is not proof.

Born: what $\psi$ means

Born's contribution to this chapter is the one without which the equation would be uninterpretable. Schrödinger had his wave and his equation; what he did not have, at first, was a secure account of what the wave was. Born supplied it in 1926: the squared modulus $|\psi|^2$ is a probability density — the probability of finding the particle in a region, not the density of a smeared-out charge. In Atomic Physics this statistical interpretation is presented as the pivot on which the whole scheme turns, the thing that lets the wave and corpuscle pictures coexist.

Born is characteristically careful about what the interpretation forbids. The wavefunction is not a physical wave in ordinary space; for two particles it is a function on a six-dimensional configuration space, which no ripple in the room could be. He resists the temptation — Schrödinger's own, at first — to see the electron as literally a packet of waves, since such a packet disperses. Born gives the equation its meaning precisely by denying $\psi$ the kind of reality that would make the meaning simpler and wrong.

Dirac: the equation was already written

For Dirac there is nothing new to introduce here, because the Schrödinger equation appeared in the previous chapter as a special case of his abstract law of motion. His fundamental equation, $i\hbar\,\frac{d}{dt}|Pt\rangle = H|Pt\rangle$, governs the state ket directly; choosing a representation in which the coordinates are diagonal, and reading $\langle\xi'|Pt\rangle$ as the wavefunction $\psi(\xi' t)$, turns that abstract equation into Schrödinger's wave equation. The wave equation is the Schrödinger picture written in the position basis — a representation of the law, not the law itself.

This inverts the usual order of exposition. For most students the Schrödinger equation is the beginning and the abstract formalism a later generalization; for Dirac the abstract equation of motion is primary and the wave equation is one of its representations, obtained by a choice of basis. The wavefunction, in this view, is not the fundamental object but the set of components of the state ket along the position eigenvectors. It is the same relationship Landau and Lifshitz build into their matrices, arrived at from the opposite end.

Synthesis

The four treatments are four answers to a single awkward question: if the Schrödinger equation cannot be derived, how should a book present it honestly? Landau and Lifshitz derive everything derivable and import the rest from the classical limit, leaving the smallest possible residue of pure postulate. Feynman motivates the equation with a concrete lattice picture and then states plainly that it was guessed. Dirac makes it a representation of a deeper axiom, so that the postulation happens elsewhere, at the level of the abstract law of motion. Born, uniquely, is not concerned with deriving the equation at all — his contribution is to fix what its solution means.

The Feynman–Landau contrast is the sharpest in Part II, and it is a contrast of temperament as much as method. Landau's instinct is that a fundamental equation should be shown to be nearly inevitable — that symmetry and correspondence leave the theorist almost no freedom. Feynman's instinct is that honesty about guessing is more valuable to a student than a derivation that only looks inevitable in hindsight. That one book reaches the equation in its third chapter of foundations and the other in its sixteenth is not a scheduling accident; it encodes two philosophies of what a wavefunction is for.

Beneath the differences they agree on the essential point, the one Born insisted on and the others build into their formalisms: $\psi$ is an instrument for computing probabilities, not a picture of a thing. The Schrödinger equation tells you how that instrument evolves. What it evolves is not a wave you could point to, and the discipline of remembering that is what separates all four of these authors from the naive reading their own equation invites.
