Where the Threads Meet: The Dirac Equation

This is the chapter the whole appendix has been walking toward, and in a sense the whole site. The main chapters told the story of quantum mechanics; the appendix told the story of relativity; and here the two theories are forced to hold at once. The result is the Dirac equation — the relativistic wave equation for the electron — and its consequences are among the most beautiful in physics. From the bare demand that quantum mechanics respect special relativity, two facts that had to be assumed in the main chapters fall out on their own: the electron has spin, and there must exist a particle with the electron's mass and the opposite charge. Antimatter was predicted on paper before it was ever seen.

Dirac leads this chapter alone, and rightly — the equation is his, and his Principles is the only one of the five books that derives it. The other quantum authors stop at the non-relativistic theory; Einstein's relativity is one of the two ingredients but not the dish. So this is not a comparison of treatments but a single author's demonstration that the two great theories of the century, joined, predict more than either alone.

Dirac's argument

The demand for a relativistic theory

Dirac opens the relativistic part of his book by naming the deficiency plainly. The theory built so far, he says, is essentially non-relativistic: it works in one Lorentz frame and is modeled on classical non-relativistic dynamics. To apply it to fast-moving particles — and the electron in an atom is fast enough to matter — the theory must be made invariant under Lorentz transformations, conforming to the special principle of relativity. General relativity, he notes, is not needed: gravitation is utterly negligible in atomic phenomena. The task is precise: find a wave equation for the electron that treats space and time on the same footing, as relativity demands.

The obstacle is subtle. The superposition principle is already relativistic, but the ordinary notion of an observable — which may refer to physical things at one instant of time — is not, because "one instant" is frame-dependent. Dirac's resolution is to insist on a representation in which the symmetry between space and time is displayed: the Schrödinger representation with the wave function written over the four coordinates $x_0, x_1, x_2, x_3$, with $x_0 = ct$. The equation he seeks must be first-order in all four, so that time enters no differently from space.

Spin, for free

The triumph of the construction is what it yields without being asked. In the main chapter on spin, all four authors had to introduce the electron's spin — Feynman from Stern-Gerlach, Dirac himself from a bare algebra of Pauli matrices, Landau and Lifshitz from rotational symmetry, Born from spectroscopic anomalies. In every case spin was an addition, grafted onto a theory that did not require it. The relativistic equation changes that entirely. Working through the motion of the electron in an electromagnetic field, Dirac finds that the equation automatically contains an extra term amounting to a magnetic moment of $-e\hbar/2mc$ — exactly the spin magnetic moment that had been assumed to explain the Zeeman effect. Pursuing the angular momentum of a free electron, he finds that the orbital angular momentum alone is not conserved; only when a spin angular momentum of $\tfrac{1}{2}\hbar\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ is added does one obtain a constant of the motion.

The electron's spin, in other words, is not an independent fact about the world that must be discovered and inserted. It is a requirement of joining quantum mechanics to relativity. Dirac is careful about the reach of the argument: it gives spin one-half to any particle whose position is an observable, which fits the electron, proton, and neutron, though not the photon — a limitation he flags rather than hides. But for the electron the conclusion is exact and unforced, and it retroactively explains why the spin chapter had felt like four authors patching the same hole. The hole was an artifact of stopping short of relativity.

Antimatter, predicted on paper

The equation had one further consequence, and it was at first an embarrassment. A relativistic energy relation is quadratic — energy appears squared — so the equation admits solutions of negative energy as well as positive. Classically these could be dismissed, but in quantum mechanics an electron could keep dropping into ever-lower negative-energy states, radiating without limit; the ordinary electron would be unstable. Dirac's resolution, the boldest stroke in the book, was to suppose that in the vacuum essentially all the negative-energy states are already filled, one electron each, so that the exclusion principle forbids an ordinary electron from falling into them. What we call empty space is this filled sea.

Then comes the prediction. Remove one electron from the negative-energy sea and the absence — the "hole" — behaves as a particle with positive energy and positive charge, the mirror image of the electron. Dirac's own fermion formalism makes the symmetry precise: the mathematics that creates a particle in an occupied state is, under an exchange of "occupied" and "unoccupied," the mathematics that creates a hole, and the holes, he writes, "are just as much physical things as the original particles." A particle with the electron's mass and the opposite charge had to exist. When Carl Anderson found the positron in cosmic rays in 1932, it was the confirmation of a particle that had been deduced, years earlier, from the requirement that Schrödinger's quantum mechanics be made consistent with Einstein's relativity.

Synthesis

The Dirac equation is where the two halves of this project meet, and the meeting is generative rather than merely consistent. Quantum mechanics and special relativity are each, on their own, complete and successful theories. Forced together, they produce what neither contains: the electron's spin as a necessity rather than an observation, and the existence of antimatter as a theorem rather than a discovery. This is the deepest vindication in the book of the conviction, which was Dirac's above all, that the mathematics is not a description laid over the physics but the physics itself — for here the mathematics knew about the positron before anyone had seen one.

It is fitting that Dirac stands alone in this final chapter, because it completes an arc that ran through the whole site. In the uncertainty chapter he reached the principle from non-commuting observables while the others used slits and wave packets; in the formalism chapter he built the abstract structure the others translated; in the spin chapter he forced the Pauli matrices out of pure algebra. Again and again his method was to trust the mathematics further than intuition would allow. The Dirac equation is the reward of that trust — the one place where following the formalism to its logical end did not merely reorganize known physics but revealed physics no one had known. The threads of the quartet and the threads of relativity meet here, in an equation that predicted a new form of matter from consistency alone.

And it ends, as the measurement chapter warned it might, on unfinished business. The Dirac equation joins quantum mechanics to special relativity; the joining of quantum mechanics to general relativity — to gravitation, to the curved spacetime of the previous chapter — has never been achieved. The quartet's theory of the very small and Einstein's theory of the very large still do not speak the same language. That the two greatest achievements of twentieth-century physics can be set side by side, and in the Dirac equation partly joined, but not yet united, is the note this comparative reading closes on. The threads meet. They are not all tied.
