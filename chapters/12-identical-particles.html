Chapter XII · Part III — The Physics

Identical Particles and the Periodic Table

Two electrons are not merely alike; they are interchangeable in a way no two classical objects can be. You cannot paint one red and follow it — the uncertainty principle forbids the tracking, so swapping two electrons produces a state that must be physically identical to the one you started with. That single demand, worked through, forces the wave function to be either symmetric or antisymmetric under exchange, splits all matter into bosons and fermions, and — through the antisymmetric case — yields the Pauli exclusion principle that builds the periodic table. It is one of the shortest paths in physics from a symmetry to the shape of chemistry.

All four authors take that path, and they agree on where it leads. The interest lies elsewhere — in how each one handles the question the path leaves open: why do half-integer-spin particles get the antisymmetric option and integer-spin particles the symmetric one? On this the chapter divides cleanly. Feynman confesses openly that he cannot explain it. Dirac, Landau and Lifshitz, and Born each state the connection and move on — but even they, tellingly, point somewhere outside their own books for the reason.

The four treatments

Feynman: the honest confession

Feynman reaches identical particles through a scattering experiment. Fire two identical particles at each other and ask for the amplitude that one arrives at a detector: there are two indistinguishable ways it can happen — the particle scattered through an angle, or its twin scattered through the supplementary angle — and the amplitudes must be combined. For some particles they add with a plus sign (bosons), for others with a minus sign (fermions), and the difference shows up directly in the scattering rate, doubling it at ninety degrees for identical bosons where classical reasoning predicts no enhancement at all.

Then Feynman does something none of the other three does: he stops and admits he cannot explain the rule. Why half-integer spin goes with the minus sign and integer spin with the plus sign is, he says, something for which they can give no elementary explanation — Pauli derived it from quantum field theory and relativity, but no one has found a simple argument, and the gap, Feynman suggests, probably means the fundamental principle is not yet completely understood. He tells the reader to take it, for now, as one of the rules of the world. It is the most striking display of intellectual honesty in the four books: at the exact point where a lesser text would assert the spin-statistics connection as though it were obvious, Feynman marks it as an open hole in the foundations.

Dirac: the assembly ket and its permutations

Dirac builds the theory from the top, as always. A system of similar particles has a state ket for the whole assembly, and interchanging any two particles is a linear operator — a permutation — acting on that ket. A ket unchanged by every permutation is symmetrical; one that keeps its sign under even permutations and flips it under odd ones is antisymmetrical. Because the Hamiltonian treats all the particles alike, a state that starts symmetrical stays symmetrical and one that starts antisymmetrical stays antisymmetrical, so nature can consistently permit only one type for a given kind of particle. The antisymmetrical ket, written as a determinant, vanishes if two particle-states coincide — and there, in one line of algebra, is the exclusion principle.

On the crucial question Dirac is matter-of-fact and empirical. He does not derive which particles are which; he reports what experiment shows. To build atoms correctly one must assume two electrons are never in the same state, so electrons are fermions; Planck's radiation law requires photons to be bosons; protons, neutrons, positrons are fermions, alpha particles bosons. Every particle in nature, he observes, appears to be one or the other, and he treats the dichotomy as a fact established in the laboratory rather than a theorem. His formalism explains everything about identical particles except the spin-statistics link, and he does not pretend otherwise — he simply grounds it in evidence instead of confessing a gap.

Landau & Lifshitz: indistinguishability and the exchange interaction

Landau and Lifshitz derive the symmetry dichotomy from the uncertainty principle itself. Because an electron has no path, localizing and numbering electrons at one instant tells you nothing about which is which an instant later; identical particles "entirely lose their individuality." Interchanging two of them can therefore change the wave function only by a phase, and repeating the interchange shows that phase must be $\pm 1$ — symmetric or antisymmetric, with no third possibility. Antisymmetric wave functions, written as a determinant, vanish when two states coincide, giving Pauli's principle; symmetric ones give Bose statistics.

They state the spin connection plainly and attribute it honestly: relativistic quantum mechanics shows that particles with half-integral spin are fermions and those with integral spin bosons — a result their non-relativistic book invokes but does not prove. Where Landau and Lifshitz go beyond the others is the exchange interaction: because the total wave function's symmetry couples the spatial part to the spin part, the energy of a system of electrons comes to depend on the total spin, even though the electric forces do not involve spin at all. This dependence is "a purely quantum effect" with no classical limit, and it is the mechanism behind the chemical bond and ferromagnetism. Their distinctive contribution is to show that indistinguishability is not just a bookkeeping rule about signs but a genuine, energy-shifting force.

Born: exclusion in the service of chemistry

Born, true to his book, comes at identical particles from the periodic table backward. His chapter runs the exclusion principle straight into the closed-shell structure of atoms: Pauli's rule that no two electrons share a state is what makes electrons stack into shells rather than all collapsing into the lowest orbit, and the shell structure is what gives the periodic system its columns — the alkalis with their single loosely-bound radiating electron, the noble gases with their filled shells, the chemical families lined up by how their outer electrons are arranged.

For Born the exclusion principle is interesting because it explains chemistry. He is less concerned with the permutation algebra that Dirac savors or the exchange energy that Landau and Lifshitz extract, and more with the payoff a chemist cares about: that the entire architecture of the periodic table — Mendeleev's columns, Moseley's ordering by atomic number, the filling of shells — follows from one principle about identical electrons. He states the spin-statistics rule as needed and spends his energy on the consequence, which is nothing less than the structure of matter as chemistry finds it. His entry point is the evidence of the elements themselves.

Synthesis

Identical particles produce the clearest four-way split in the project on a single question: not what the rule is, but why it holds. Feynman confesses he cannot explain the spin-statistics connection and names it an open problem. Dirac asserts the boson-fermion dichotomy as an experimental fact. Landau and Lifshitz assert it too and attribute it to relativistic quantum mechanics. Born states it and turns immediately to what it explains. Three assertions and one confession — and the confession is the most honest moment in the four books, because it refuses to dress an unexplained rule as an obvious one.

Yet the three assertions are more honest than they first appear, and that is the chapter's quiet lesson. Dirac grounds the dichotomy in the laboratory, not in his formalism; Landau and Lifshitz point explicitly to relativistic quantum mechanics, a theory outside their volume; Born leans on the spectroscopic and chemical evidence. None of the four claims to derive the spin-statistics connection from what is in front of the reader, because none of them can — it requires quantum field theory, which lies beyond all four books. What separates Feynman is not that he knows less but that he says so loudest, converting a shared limitation into an explicit lesson about the edge of understanding.

What they share is the destination, and it is a remarkable one. From the bare fact that electrons cannot be told apart, each author reaches — by permutation algebra, by phase argument, by scattering amplitude, by shell-filling — the same antisymmetric wave function, the same exclusion principle, and the same periodic table. That a symmetry of indistinguishability should dictate the form of the chemical elements is one of the deepest results in the subject, and the four books converge on it from four directions. They differ only, and revealingly, in how candidly each admits where the road runs out.
