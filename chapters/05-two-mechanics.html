Two Mechanics: Matrices vs. Waves

For about a year, quantum mechanics existed in two apparently unrelated forms. In 1925, Heisenberg, Born, and Jordan built a mechanics out of arrays of numbers — matrix mechanics — in which observable quantities were represented by matrices and the act of measurement had no picture attached to it at all. In 1926, Schrödinger published a wave equation for a continuous function $\psi$ that looked reassuringly like the mathematics of vibrating strings and diffusing heat. The two schemes gave the same answers and shared no obvious common ground. Then Schrödinger, and independently Dirac, showed they were the same theory.

This chapter is about that strange interval and its resolution. It is the one chapter where the comparison is partly historical, and the four authors divide along a fault line: two of them lived the events and two received them as finished mathematics. Born was a co-inventor of matrix mechanics; his account is testimony. Dirac supplies the cleanest statement of why the two forms are one. Landau and Lifshitz absorb both into a single formalism from the first page. Feynman scarcely mentions the episode — and that silence is itself a data point.

The four treatments

Born: the participant's account

Born is the only one of the four who can write about matrix mechanics in the first person, and Atomic Physics quietly does. He recounts Heisenberg's 1925 starting point — the demand that a consistent atomic mechanics admit only observable quantities, the frequencies and intensities of emitted light, and not the unobservable orbit of an electron inside the atom. From that principle Heisenberg was led to represent each quantity by an array indexed by pairs of states, and to a rule for multiplying two such arrays.

The decisive recognition, Born notes, was his own and Jordan's: Heisenberg's multiplication rule was exactly the rule mathematicians already knew for multiplying matrices. What Heisenberg had reinvented from spectroscopic data was a piece of nineteenth-century linear algebra, and the non-commuting products that had puzzled him were simply the ordinary non-commutativity of matrix multiplication. Born's telling is generous and precise about who did what, and it carries the authority of a man describing a room he was standing in. He is also unhurried about the formal development — he omits much of it, since by the time he is writing, wave mechanics has become the working language and matrix mechanics is the historical root.

His verdict on the two schemes is the one that matters: though entirely different in form, they are, as Schrödinger showed, identical in content. Born states this as settled fact, not as a claim he needs to defend, because he watched it become settled.

Dirac: equivalence as a change of picture

Dirac gives the argument Born only gestures at. In his hands the two "mechanics" become two pictures of a single theory, related by a unitary transformation. In the Schrödinger picture the state is a moving ket $|Pt\rangle$, carried along in time by the Hamiltonian, while the observables sit fixed. Apply a time-dependent unitary transformation $T$ that brings the states of undisturbed motion to rest, and the motion is transferred instead onto the observables, which become moving operators — this is the Heisenberg picture.

Because the transformation is unitary, every algebraic relation is preserved: the two pictures are not merely compatible, they are the same physics wearing different clothes. Dirac's Heisenberg-picture equation of motion for an observable, $\dot v_t = [v_t, H_t]$ in his bracket notation, sits beside the classical Hamiltonian equation of the identical form, which is exactly why he can call the energy operator the "Hamiltonian" and carry classical language into the quantum theory. He then remarks, drily, which picture to actually compute in: the Schrödinger form gives the simpler equations and fewer unknowns, while the Heisenberg form is valued for making the analogy with classical mechanics visible. The equivalence is not a historical reconciliation for Dirac; it is a theorem about a transformation.

Landau & Lifshitz: no two mechanics to reconcile

Landau and Lifshitz dissolve the problem before it can arise. In their presentation the operator attached to a physical quantity is primary, and a matrix is nothing more than that operator written out in a particular basis: the matrix element $f_{nm} = \int \psi_n^* \hat f \psi_m \, dq$ is the operator's component between two stationary states. Matrix multiplication is then not a postulate but a consequence — it falls directly out of composing two operators and expanding in a complete set. What Heisenberg discovered as a mysterious rule, they derive in a line.

They do acknowledge the history, but only in a footnote: the matrix representation was introduced by Heisenberg in 1925, before Schrödinger's wave equation, and matrix mechanics was developed by Born, Heisenberg, and Jordan. In the body of the text there is no drama of two rival theories, because from their starting point there was only ever one. The "energy representation," in which the Hamiltonian matrix is diagonal and the diagonal entries are the energy levels, is presented as one convenient choice of basis among many, related to others by the unitary transformations of their §12. Where Born narrates and Dirac proves, Landau and Lifshitz simply build the house so that the question does not come up.

Feynman: the eloquent silence

Feynman's Volume III has almost nothing to say about the events of 1925–26, and the omission is deliberate. He tells the reader plainly that the traditional course retraces the historical path — classical mechanics first, then the Schrödinger equation, then, much later, the "advanced" topics — and that he is abandoning that path on purpose. His wager is that the amplitude formalism, taken as the starting point, makes the historical scaffolding unnecessary. The matrix-versus-wave controversy was a stage the subject passed through on its way to a clean formulation, and Feynman elects to begin at the destination.

So the cell in the comparison table for Feynman is intentionally near-empty. It records a pedagogical stance: that the equivalence of the two mechanics, which cost the founders real effort to establish, is invisible to a student who is never shown the two forms as separate in the first place. What looks like silence is a claim — that the history is not load-bearing.

Synthesis

The four treatments line up as four distances from a single event. Born stands inside it, reporting what was done and by whom. Dirac stands just outside, holding the transformation that shows why the two forms had to coincide. Landau and Lifshitz stand far enough away that the two forms have merged into one formalism with a choice of basis. Feynman stands so far downstream that he can decline to mention the fork at all. No one of these vantage points is more correct; together they show a theory hardening from lived controversy into textbook architecture.

There is a lesson in the sequence about how physics digests its own revolutions. The matrix–wave duality was, for a year, a genuine mystery: two calculational engines with no common parts, agreeing on every answer. The resolution — that they are unitary images of each other — did not just settle a priority dispute; it revealed the object underneath both, the abstract state and its operators, which is what Dirac and then Landau and Lifshitz take as their true subject. Born's history and Feynman's silence are the two ends of that process: the memory of the mystery, and the confidence to skip it.

It is worth noticing what the equivalence cost and what it bought. It cost the intuition that a wave function is a wave in space — an intuition the previous chapter already showed all four authors refusing. It bought the freedom to move between pictures at will, computing in whichever representation is easiest and trusting that the physics is representation-independent. That freedom is the quiet foundation of everything in Part III.
