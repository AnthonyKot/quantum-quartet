Appendix B · Relativity

Mass, Energy, Relativistic Dynamics

The most famous equation in physics, $E = mc^2$, is a consequence of the Lorentz transformation, not a separate postulate. Once space and time are reworked so that the laws of physics respect the constancy of light, mechanics must be reworked too: momentum and energy take new forms, mass grows with velocity, and — the startling result — the inertia of a body turns out to be a measure of its energy content. Rest mass is just frozen energy. This chapter follows how Einstein and Feynman each arrive at that identity.

Both reach the same conclusion — that the conservation of mass and the conservation of energy are one law, not two — and both reach it by insisting that relativity be consistent. But the routes differ in a way that has become familiar across this site. Einstein argues from the energy a body absorbs; Feynman argues from what happens when relativistic objects collide. The discoverer reasons from a principle; the teacher reasons from a crash.

The two treatments

Einstein: mass as a measure of energy

Einstein reaches the mass–energy relation by asking what happens to a body's inertia when it absorbs energy. Working from the relativistic expression for the energy of a moving body, he shows that a body which takes up an amount of energy $E_0$ has its inertial mass increased by $E_0/c^2$. The inference he draws is sweeping: the inertial mass of a body is not a constant but varies with its energy content, so that the inertial mass of a system can be regarded as a measure of its energy. The law of conservation of mass and the law of conservation of energy merge into a single law — valid, he is careful to add, only so long as the system neither absorbs nor emits energy.

The term $mc^2$, he then observes, is nothing other than the energy the body already possessed before it absorbed anything — the rest energy. Einstein is scrupulous about the empirical status of the claim. Writing in 1920, he notes that a direct experimental test is not yet possible, because the energy changes one can impose on a system are far too small to register as a measurable change in its mass — which is precisely why classical physics had been able to treat conservation of mass as an independent law. A footnote added to the later edition records the vindication tersely: the equation has since been thoroughly proved, time and again. The relation is, for Einstein, an unforced consequence of the relativistic energy expression, and its smallness in everyday terms explains why it went unnoticed for so long.

Feynman: mass from the collision

Feynman derives the same identity from dynamics. Having established that mass depends on velocity, he shows that the work done by forces on a body changes its kinetic energy by $\Delta T = (m - m_0)c^2$, and then makes the bolder guess that the total energy of a body is its total mass times $c^2$. To test the idea he turns to an inelastic collision. When two equal masses stick together, the composite is heavier than the sum of the rest masses — heavier by exactly the kinetic energy brought into the collision. Energy has inertia; a hotter gas is a heavier gas; a compound assembled forcefully weighs more than the same pieces put together gently.

From this Feynman draws a conclusion he clearly relishes: there is no place in relativity for a strictly inelastic collision of the Newtonian kind. In Newtonian mechanics two bodies could collide and form an object indistinguishable from one assembled slowly, the extra kinetic energy leaving the mass untouched. Relativity forbids it — the energy of the collision shows up as extra mass, so the resulting object is genuinely different and measurably heavier. The conservation of mass, he concludes, must go along with the conservation of energy; they are the same principle. Where Einstein infers the identity from the energy a body absorbs, Feynman forces it out of the books balancing in a collision, and makes the disappearance of the Newtonian inelastic collision the vivid sign that something fundamental has changed.

Synthesis

Mass–energy equivalence is the rare result that looks the same from every angle, and the two authors differ only in which face they present first. Einstein leads with absorption: give a body energy and its inertia grows, so mass measures energy. Feynman leads with collision: smash bodies together and the debris is heavier, so energy has mass. Both then state the unifying conclusion in nearly identical words — the conservation of mass and the conservation of energy are one law — and both note that the effect is invisible in daily life only because $c^2$ is enormous and the mass changes correspondingly tiny.

The chapter also shows the two temperaments that have run through the whole site, transplanted to a settled subject. Einstein reasons from a principle to its consequence and is meticulous about what experiment can and cannot yet confirm — the same care he took with simultaneity. Feynman reasons from a concrete physical situation, a collision he can picture, and prizes the moment when the new physics contradicts the old in a way you could in principle weigh on a scale. That the discoverer and the teacher reach $E = mc^2$ by such different roads, and disagree about nothing at the end, is the appendix's quiet counterpoint to the quantum chapters, where the roads diverge and the destinations sometimes do too. Here there is one summit and two paths up.
