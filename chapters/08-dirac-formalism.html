Chapter VIII · Part II — The New Mechanics

States, Observables, Bras and Kets

By this point in the story the wave function has stopped being the fundamental object and become a shadow of one — the components of an abstract state along the position axis. The formalism that makes that reading precise is Dirac's: states are vectors, written as kets $|A\rangle$; measurable quantities are operators called observables; and the pairing of a bra with a ket, $\langle B|A\rangle$, is the complex amplitude that ties the whole theory to experiment. This chapter is about that formalism and the different relationships our four authors have to it.

Here the roles are unusually clear. Dirac is the author; the notation and the architecture are his invention, and the chapter is in a real sense about his book. The other three become a translation layer, each rendering the same structure in a different dialect. Feynman adopts Dirac's brackets wholesale but reaches them through Stern-Gerlach filters instead of axioms. Landau and Lifshitz use much of the same algebra while pointedly keeping the wave function, not the ket, as their primary object — and the places where their notation diverges from Dirac's are worth marking exactly. Born has no bra-ket formalism at all.

The four treatments

Dirac: the architecture

Dirac builds the formalism from the top down, and every later author is reacting to his sequence. A state is a ket vector; to each ket he pairs a dual object, a bra $\langle B|$, so that the scalar product $\langle B|A\rangle$ is a single complex number — the notation itself splits the word "bracket" across the two halves. A dynamical variable becomes a linear operator, and the physically meaningful operators are the real (Hermitian) ones, whose eigenvalues are real numbers.

His deepest definition is of an observable. A real dynamical variable counts as observable only if its eigenstates form a complete set — if any state can be expanded in them. This is not a technicality; it is what lets measurement work. Dirac argues that measuring a variable makes the system "jump" into one of its eigenstates, with the measured value being the corresponding eigenvalue, so the possible results of a measurement are exactly the eigenvalues, and a repeated measurement immediately after must give the same answer. The projector $|A\rangle\langle A|$ is itself an observable — the one that asks whether the system is in the state $|A\rangle$, with eigenvalues 1 and 0. Superposition, orthogonality, and measurement all acquire their physical meaning inside this one scheme.

What Dirac achieves is a language in which the wave equation, the matrices, and the measurement postulate are all shadows of a single vector-space structure. It is the most economical of the four presentations and the least tethered to any picture — which is its power and, for a first-time reader, its difficulty.

Feynman: the same brackets, earned from filters

Feynman uses Dirac's notation without apology — the amplitude $\langle\chi|\phi\rangle$ runs through all of Volume III — but he refuses to introduce it as an axiom. His route is the Stern-Gerlach apparatus: a device that splits a beam by spin and can be used to filter, block, or recombine base states. From manipulating such filters he extracts the completeness relation, $\langle\chi|\phi\rangle = \sum_i \langle\chi|i\rangle\langle i|\phi\rangle$, as a physical statement about passing through a complete set of intermediate channels — and then remarks that the equation holds whether or not the apparatus is actually there. The formula outlives the picture that motivated it.

His analogy for the state is deliberately homely: the base-state amplitudes $\langle i|\phi\rangle$ are like the components of an ordinary vector along chosen axes, and knowing them is knowing everything about the state — which is why, he notes, a "state" is also called a "state vector." Feynman thus arrives at exactly Dirac's structure, complete with orthonormal base states $\langle i|j\rangle = \delta_{ij}$ and the resolution $|\phi\rangle = \sum_i |i\rangle\langle i|\phi\rangle$, but the reader who follows him has handled a beam of atoms the whole way. He is the translation layer that keeps a thumb on the experiment.

Landau & Lifshitz: the same algebra, a different primary object

Landau and Lifshitz use a great deal of the same machinery — Hermitian operators, eigenvalues, complete sets, matrix elements — and they even adopt Dirac's bracket as a notation: they write the matrix element of a quantity $f$ as $\langle n|f|m\rangle$, equal to their $f_{nm} = \int \psi_n^* \hat f \psi_m\,dq$. But the divergence from Dirac is real and worth stating precisely. For Dirac the ket is the fundamental object and the wave function is its component along a position eigenket; for Landau and Lifshitz the wave function is primary and the bracket is shorthand for an integral over it. The abstract state vector, which Dirac takes as bedrock, never becomes their basic object; it is Feynman's and Dirac's "state vector," not theirs.

The practical consequence shows in what each treats as a definition versus a derived formula. Dirac defines the scalar product $\langle B|A\rangle$ abstractly and lets integrals appear only when he chooses a representation. Landau and Lifshitz define $\langle n|f|m\rangle$ as the integral, so the bracket is a compact name for something more concrete rather than a primitive. The algebra of matrix multiplication, transformation between representations, and the trace are all present in their §§11–13, but they arise as properties of operators acting on wave functions, not as the axioms of an abstract space. It is the same country reached by a different road, and their map keeps the wave function at the center where Dirac keeps the ket.

Born: formalism only where a problem demands it

Born's Atomic Physics has no chapter on the abstract formalism, and that is consistent with the book's whole character. Operators appear when a specific problem needs them — the Pauli spin matrices when he treats the spinning electron, the differential operator $\hat p = -i\hbar\,\partial_x$ for momentum in wave mechanics — but never as an axiomatic system introduced for its own sake. There are no kets and no bras. The commutation relations he mentions are presented as facts about these particular operators, not as the founding structure of the theory.

This is not a gap so much as a different genre. Born is writing a physics book organized around phenomena and their explanation, and he reaches for whatever mathematical tool the phenomenon requires. The abstract vector-space formulation, which for Dirac is the whole point, is for Born an implicit background he can invoke piecewise. His cell in the comparison table is nearly empty of "axiom" and "thought experiment" for the same reason his uncertainty chapter led with a slit: he trusts the concrete instance over the general scheme.

Synthesis

This chapter measures four distances from a single invention. Dirac made the formalism, and for him it is the theory itself — states are vectors, observables are operators with complete eigenstates, and physics is the algebra of brackets. Feynman adopts the invention entire but insists on earning each piece from an experiment with atoms and magnets, so that the abstraction never floats free of the laboratory. Landau and Lifshitz borrow the notation and most of the algebra while quietly refusing the central ontological move: for them the wave function, not the ket, is what is real, and the bracket is a convenience. Born reaches for operators only when a calculation demands one and never builds the general scheme at all.

The most instructive comparison is between Feynman and Landau and Lifshitz, because both use Dirac's brackets and yet mean different things by them. In Feynman the bracket is an amplitude — a physical quantity you could in principle measure the square of — motivated by filters and beams. In Landau and Lifshitz the identical symbol $\langle n|f|m\rangle$ is defined as an integral, a piece of bookkeeping over wave functions. A student who moves between the two books without noticing this will read the same marks on the page as two different objects. The notation converged; the underlying commitments did not.

What the chapter reveals is that a formalism is never neutral. Dirac's brackets look like pure convenience — and Feynman and Landau both take advantage of that convenience — but adopting them fully, as Dirac does, means accepting that the abstract state is the primary reality and the wave function a mere representation. Landau and Lifshitz take the notation and decline the metaphysics; Born declines both. The choice of what to treat as fundamental is made not in the equations but in the decision about where to begin, and on that decision the four authors do not agree.
