The Bohr Atom and the Old Quantum Theory

Niels Bohr's model of the atom, published in 1913, is the most successful failure in the history of physics. It explained the hydrogen spectrum — a puzzle that had resisted every attempt at a classical solution — and it did so with two postulates that were incompatible with classical physics. The model was wrong, but it was the right kind of wrong: it pointed toward the theory that would replace it.

Bohr's atom consists of a nucleus orbited by electrons in discrete circular orbits. Electrons can jump between orbits, emitting or absorbing a photon with energy equal to the difference between the orbits: $h\nu = E_i - E_f$. The orbits themselves are selected by the quantization condition $mvr = n\hbar$, where $n$ is an integer.

This is the "old quantum theory" — a patchwork of quantization rules applied to classical orbits. It works for hydrogen, fails for helium, and cannot explain why the quantization condition should hold. It is a scaffold, not a building.

Born's treatment

Born treats the Bohr atom as a major achievement in Atomic Physics, but he is clear-eyed about its limitations. He explains the model, derives the hydrogen energy levels, and shows how the Balmer formula follows from Bohr's postulates. But he also names the questions it leaves unexplained: why certain orbits must be excluded on grounds outside the theory itself, why the anomalous Zeeman effect resists it (the explanation, he notes, will require the electron's own spin), and how the helium problem — the simplest system with more than one electron — yields results contrary to experiment.

Born's treatment is notable for its emphasis on the correspondence principle: the idea that quantum mechanics must reproduce classical physics in the limit of large quantum numbers. This principle guided Bohr himself and was one of the key insights that led to matrix mechanics. Born understood its importance because he was one of the people who used it.

The near-absence of Feynman, Landau & Lifshitz, and Dirac

Feynman mentions the Bohr atom in passing, as a historical curiosity. He does not treat it as a serious theory worth studying in its own right. This is characteristic of Feynman's approach: he prefers to teach the correct theory directly, without getting bogged down in the detours that led to it.

Landau and Lifshitz skip the old quantum theory almost entirely. Their quantum mechanics book begins with the uncertainty principle and the Schrödinger equation, which provide a complete and consistent framework. There is no need to revisit the scaffolding.

Dirac is similarly silent. The old quantum theory was, in his view, a collection of ad hoc rules without a coherent foundation. It was not worth preserving in a book that aimed to present quantum mechanics from first principles.

This triple near-absence is worth reflecting on. The old quantum theory was the bridge between Planck's quanta and the full theory of quantum mechanics. It was a scaffold, not a building — and three of the four authors treated it as such. Born, who lived through the period, preserved it as a historical record. The others recognized that it had served its purpose and moved on.

The legacy of the Bohr atom

Despite its failures, the Bohr atom left lasting legacies. The concept of energy levels — discrete states of a bound system — is central to quantum mechanics. The idea that transitions between states involve the emission or absorption of a photon is fundamental. The correspondence principle became a guiding heuristic in the development of the theory.

But the Bohr model's greatest contribution was pedagogical. It showed that quantum effects could be calculated in concrete situations — that the abstract idea of quantization could be applied to a real physical system and yield testable predictions. This was the spark that made quantum mechanics seem not just philosophically interesting but scientifically productive.

Synthesis

The chapter on the Bohr atom teaches two lessons. The first is that science advances through failures as well as successes. The Bohr model was wrong, but it was the right kind of wrong — it pointed toward the correct theory and provided the tools (energy levels, transitions, the correspondence principle) that the correct theory inherited.

The second lesson is about the difference between history and foundations. Born treats the old quantum theory as a historical record because he remembers it. Feynman, Landau and Lifshitz, and Dirac treat it as a foundational detour because they do not need it. Both perspectives are valid, and both are valuable.

The old quantum theory was a scaffold. Scaffolds are meant to be removed. The question is whether removing them erases the memory of how the building was constructed — and whether that memory is worth preserving.
