Chapter VII · Part II — The New Mechanics

The Uncertainty Principle

The uncertainty principle is the first thing a student learns about quantum mechanics and the last thing she truly understands. It sounds like a limitation — "we cannot measure position and momentum simultaneously with arbitrary precision" — but each of our four authors writes about it as something deeper: a structural feature of reality, not a statement about measurement.

What makes this chapter interesting is how differently the four books arrive at the same inequality. Born reaches it through diffraction at a single slit, then sharpens it with a γ-ray microscope. Feynman derives it from the double-slit experiment, as a consequence of superposition. Landau and Lifshitz build it from the operator formalism, as a theorem. Dirac states it from non-commuting observables, as a consequence of the quantum conditions.

The four treatments

Born: diffraction at a slit, then the microscope

Born does not open Atomic Physics with the uncertainty principle. It arrives well into the book, in the chapter on wave-corpuscles, under the heading "the contradiction between the wave theory and the corpuscular theory, and its removal." The framing is telling: for Born the uncertainty relation is not a starting axiom but the resolution of a paradox — the price of holding the wave and particle pictures together.

His primary derivation is concrete and experimental. Send an electron through a single slit of width $\Delta x$. The slit fixes the position to within $\Delta x$; but diffraction spreads the beam, and from the corpuscular standpoint that spread is a transverse momentum $\Delta p$ acquired at the slit. Combining the slit width with the de Broglie relation $\lambda = h/p$ gives $\Delta x \, \Delta p \sim h$ directly. Born reaches the inequality by contrasting the two descriptions of the same experiment, not by manipulating operators.

He then reinforces it with a second, independent argument: Heisenberg's γ-ray microscope. To locate an electron optically you must scatter light of short enough wavelength off it, but short wavelength means a large Compton recoil — so sharper position costs a larger momentum disturbance. Only as a third example does Born turn to wave packets, noting that building a spatially bounded packet requires a finite range of momenta. The rigorous inequality he defers to an appendix. This ordering — slit first, microscope second, wave packets third — is the opposite of the "Born is the wave-packet man" caricature, and it is worth getting right.

Feynman: the double-slit

Feynman's treatment in Volume III of the Lectures is arguably the most famous of the four. He opens with the double-slit experiment — not as a historical curiosity but as the essential phenomenon of quantum mechanics. Everything, he insists, comes back to the interference pattern of two slits.

From the double slit, Feynman derives the uncertainty principle. If you try to determine which slit the particle went through, you disturb its momentum enough to destroy the interference pattern. The argument is not a proof so much as a demonstration — a way of seeing why the uncertainty principle must hold, grounded in a concrete experimental situation.

Feynman's style is deliberately informal. He does not introduce Hilbert spaces or operators at this stage. He introduces probability amplitudes, superposition, and the idea that quantum particles behave in ways that no classical picture can capture. The uncertainty principle is a consequence of this deeper fact, not a starting point.

Landau & Lifshitz: the commutator proof

Landau and Lifshitz open their entire volume with a section titled "the uncertainty principle" — but, revealingly, that opening section contains no inequality. It begins with electron diffraction and a long meditation on the role of the classical measuring apparatus: quantum mechanics, they argue, contains classical mechanics as a limiting case yet requires it for its own formulation. The principle is introduced first as a statement about what cannot be simultaneously measured, not as a formula.

The actual inequality arrives later, once the machinery is in place. With position and momentum promoted to Hermitian operators obeying the canonical commutation relation $[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$, the relation $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \ge \hbar/2$ follows from the operator formalism as a theorem. There is no appeal to a slit or a microscope — the physical picture was discharged in the opening section, and what remains is structure.

This split is characteristic. Landau and Lifshitz separate the conceptual content of the principle (why measurement is limited) from its mathematical content (the inequality between conjugate operators), and they refuse to let the first masquerade as a derivation of the second. It is the most disciplined of the four treatments about not confusing a picture with a proof.

Dirac: non-commuting observables

Dirac's treatment in the Principles of Quantum Mechanics is the most abstract of the four. His book does open with a physical discussion — the polarization and interference of photons — but by the time he reaches the uncertainty principle, deep into the chapter on the quantum conditions, the physics has been fully absorbed into algebra. The controlling fact is that certain pairs of observables, position and momentum among them, do not commute.

For Dirac, this non-commutativity is primitive. The uncertainty relation follows from it as a consequence of the quantum conditions, not from any thought experiment about slits or microscopes. His presentation is famously terse — a single sentence can carry a result that takes others a page — and it reflects his conviction that, properly set up, the mathematics is the physics.

Dirac's approach has a certain purity that the other treatments lack, but it also has a cost: it is less connected to physical intuition. A student who reads only Dirac might understand the algebra of the uncertainty principle without ever having a feeling for why it matters.

Synthesis

What do we learn from reading these four treatments side by side?

The first lesson is that the uncertainty principle is not a single idea but a cluster of ideas that happen to be expressed by the same formula. Born sees it as what forces the wave and particle pictures into a truce — read off a diffracting slit. Feynman sees it as a consequence of superposition. Landau and Lifshitz see it as a theorem of operator algebra. Dirac sees it as a consequence of non-commuting observables. None of these views is wrong; they are complementary.

The second lesson is about pedagogy. Born's single-slit argument is the easiest to picture physically but the hardest to generalize. Feynman's double-slit argument is the most vivid but the hardest to formalize. Landau and Lifshitz's commutator proof is the most general but the least intuitive. Dirac's non-commuting observables is the most elegant but the least grounded. Each treatment illuminates something the others obscure.

The third lesson — and the one that matters most — is that the uncertainty principle is not primarily about measurement. Born's diffraction pattern forms whether or not anyone measures which slit the electron took. Feynman's double-slit interference pattern exists whether or not anyone looks at it. Landau and Lifshitz's commutator is a property of operators, not of measuring devices. Dirac's non-commuting observables are a mathematical fact. The uncertainty principle is a statement about the structure of quantum states, not about the limitations of our instruments.

This is the difference between the popular understanding of the uncertainty principle (as a statement about measurement disturbance) and what the four authors actually teach. The popular understanding is not wrong — it captures a real aspect of the physics — but it misses the deeper point: that quantum mechanics simply does not assign simultaneous definite values to position and momentum, regardless of whether anyone measures them.
