Appendix C · Relativity

General Relativity, Sketched

This chapter is a sketch, and says so plainly. General relativity — Einstein's theory of gravitation as the curvature of spacetime — is the one major subject in this project that none of the five books treats at a level a general reader can follow with the mathematics in front of them. The full theory requires tensor calculus and Riemannian geometry that lie well beyond the scope of a comparative reading. What can be conveyed, and what Einstein's 1916 popular exposition conveys beautifully, is the physical idea at the theory's root: that gravitation and acceleration are locally indistinguishable, and that this equivalence forces spacetime to be curved.

Here the comparison thins to almost a single voice. Einstein leads, as the theory's author writing for the public. Feynman's Volume I, from which the previous two appendix chapters drew, does not develop general relativity — it belongs to a later volume and a harder treatment. The four quantum authors are, of course, silent on it entirely; their subject is the very small, and gravitation plays no role in non-relativistic quantum mechanics. So this chapter presents Einstein's central argument and is honest about stopping where the mathematics would begin.

Einstein's argument

The equivalence principle

Einstein's way into general relativity is the single most famous thought experiment in physics, and his popular book presents it with disarming clarity. Imagine a large chest, far from any star, with an observer inside; gravitation does not exist for him, and he floats. Now suppose a "being" attaches a rope to the outside of the chest and pulls with a constant force, so that the chest accelerates uniformly. The observer inside is pressed to the floor exactly as he would be standing in a room on Earth. If he releases a body, it falls to the floor with an acceleration that is the same whatever body he chooses — precisely the hallmark of a gravitational field.

The observer, Einstein argues, is entitled to conclude that he sits at rest in a uniform gravitational field, with the chest suspended by the rope. Ought we to correct him? Einstein's answer is that we ought not — his interpretation violates neither reason nor any known mechanical law. There is no experiment the man can perform inside the chest to distinguish "I am being accelerated" from "I am at rest in a gravitational field." This indistinguishability, resting on the observed equality of inertial and gravitational mass, is the equivalence principle, and it is the ground on which Einstein extends the principle of relativity from uniform motion to accelerated frames — the step from special to general relativity.

Why spacetime must curve

From the equivalence principle Einstein draws consequences that a reader can grasp without the field equations. Because acceleration and gravitation are locally the same, a gravitational field must bend light and slow clocks — a beam crossing the accelerating chest appears to curve, so a beam crossing a real gravitational field must curve too. His most vivid geometrical argument is the rotating disc. Set a large disc spinning and measure it with rods: the circumference, whose rods are contracted by their motion, no longer stands to the diameter in the ratio $\pi$. Euclid's geometry fails on the rotating disc — and since, by the equivalence principle, rotation is a kind of gravitational field, geometry itself must be non-Euclidean wherever gravitation is present.

This is as far as a sketch honestly reaches. The conclusion — that gravitation is not a force acting within a flat space but the curvature of spacetime itself, and that matter tells spacetime how to curve while spacetime tells matter how to move — is stated by Einstein in words but established only through the mathematics of Gaussian coordinates and the general field equations, which his popular book gently approaches and this chapter does not. The reader who wants the physical intuition has it here; the reader who wants the theory must go to the sources, and past them.

Synthesis

The honest content of this chapter is a single, powerful idea presented by its author, and an acknowledgment of a limit. Einstein's equivalence principle — that a freely falling observer feels no gravity, and an accelerated one cannot tell acceleration from gravitation — is the conceptual seed of general relativity, and it is fully graspable without mathematics. That is why his 1916 exposition remains, a century on, one of the best first encounters with the idea. What cannot be sketched is the harvest: the field equations, the bending of starlight, the precession of Mercury, the expansion of the universe. Those require the machinery the popular book can only gesture toward.

It is worth marking why the comparison collapses here to one voice, because the silence is itself informative. General relativity stands apart from everything else in this project: it is a classical field theory of gravitation, and it has never been reconciled with the quantum mechanics that fills the main chapters. The four quantum authors do not treat it because their domain — atoms, electrons, the very small — is one where gravitation is utterly negligible. Feynman treats it, but elsewhere and harder. The gap between this appendix and the rest of the site is the same gap that still runs through physics itself: the unfinished business of joining the theory of the very large to the theory of the very small. That this comparative reading can set them side by side but not unite them is not a failure of the reading. It is the honest state of the subject.
