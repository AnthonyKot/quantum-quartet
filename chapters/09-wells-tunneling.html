Chapter IX · Part III — The Physics

Wells, Barriers, and Tunneling

Give a classical particle less energy than the wall in front of it and it stops; it cannot be found on the far side. Quantum mechanics denies this flatly. The wave function does not vanish at a barrier — it decays through it, and if the barrier is thin enough, a wave emerges on the other side. A particle can appear where classical energy conservation forbids it. This is tunneling, and it is the first place in the book where the abstract formalism of Part II pays out in a concrete, measurable, deeply non-classical effect.

This is a calculational chapter, and it belongs to Landau. His book treats wells, steps, and barriers as a systematic catalogue of exactly solvable problems, deriving transmission and reflection coefficients from first principles. Born reaches tunneling from the opposite end — through the physical phenomenon that made it famous, the alpha decay of radioactive nuclei. Dirac, whose book is about structure rather than specific potentials, does not treat it. Feynman's Volume III has no tunneling chapter at all, and the gap is worth naming precisely.

The four treatments

Landau & Lifshitz: the systematic catalogue

Landau and Lifshitz treat barrier problems the way they treat everything — by setting up the machinery once and then turning the crank. They define the transmission coefficient $D$ as the ratio of transmitted to incident probability current, and the reflection coefficient $R = 1 - D$, and derive both by matching wave functions across the potential. For a step, a particle with energy above the wall can still be reflected — a phenomenon with no classical analogue that falls straight out of the wave matching. For a rectangular barrier of width $a$ with $E < U_0$, the transmitted amplitude decays exponentially and the transmission coefficient carries the tell-tale $e^{-2\kappa a}$ suppression, where $\hbar\kappa = \sqrt{2m(U_0 - E)}$.

What is distinctive is how far they push exact solvability. Rather than settling for the quasi-classical (WKB) estimate, they solve several smoothly varying barriers exactly, in terms of hypergeometric functions — the potential $U_0/\cosh^2\alpha x$, the smooth step $U_0/(1 + e^{-\alpha x})$ — and read the transmission coefficient off the asymptotics. Along the way they turn up genuine surprises: a particle passing over a well of the right depth is not reflected at all, a resonance condition invisible to any classical picture. The quasi-classical penetration formula, the one most physicists remember, they present later as an approximation to be used when the exact solution is out of reach. For Landau the barrier is not a curiosity but a proving ground for technique.

Born: tunneling as the escape of an alpha particle

Born comes at tunneling through the phenomenon that first demanded it. In 1928 Gamow, and independently Condon and Gurney, explained radioactive alpha decay as barrier penetration, and Born makes their account the centrepiece. He pictures the alpha particle held inside the nucleus by a potential shaped like a crater: the Coulomb repulsion outside forms a rim far higher than the energy of the escaping particle, so that classically the alpha particle, once inside, could never get out. Yet it does — with a half-life that can be geological.

The wave-mechanical resolution is exactly the barrier physics Landau formalizes, but Born keeps it tethered to the nucleus. The standing wave inside the crater is not extinguished at the wall; it leaks through and emerges outside as a travelling wave, and $|\psi|^2$ at the outer edge sets the probability of escape. Because that probability falls off steeply with barrier thickness, fast alpha particles escape far more readily than slow ones — which, Born notes, is the qualitative content of the Geiger–Nuttall law relating decay rate to alpha energy. He defers the exact formula to an appendix and stresses what the model needs and does not need: essentially only the nuclear radius at the rim. He then extends the same idea to the cold emission of electrons from a metal under a strong field, another barrier the electron has no classical right to cross. Where Landau derives the coefficient, Born shows you the crater and the decaying nucleus it explains.

Dirac: not this kind of problem

Dirac's Principles does not treat wells and barriers, and the omission is a matter of genre rather than oversight. His book is built around the abstract structure of the theory — states, observables, the algebra of the transformation theory — and it descends to specific potentials only for the few systems that illuminate the general method, chiefly the free particle and the harmonic oscillator. A rectangular barrier is a boundary-value problem in a particular $U(x)$, precisely the kind of concrete calculation Dirac leaves to books written in a different key. His absence here is the same austerity that made his uncertainty chapter start from non-commuting observables rather than a slit.

Feynman: an absence in the table of contents

Feynman's Volume III contains no chapter on tunneling. This is not a rough characterization but a fact about the table of contents: the volume moves from spin and two-state systems through the amplitude formalism to the hydrogen atom, and a barrier-penetration problem never gets its own treatment. The physics of tunneling is not absent from Feynman's thought — it appears in Volume III's discussion of two-state systems and, elsewhere in the Lectures, in solid-state contexts — but the canonical square-barrier calculation that anchors Landau's chapter has no counterpart in the quantum-mechanics volume.

The cell is worth leaving visibly near-empty, because it marks a real difference in what each book is for. Landau's volume is, among other things, a reference catalogue of solved problems, and a barrier is one of the entries. Feynman's is a conceptual tour built to convey how quantum mechanics thinks, and a barrier calculation, however important, is not on the itinerary. The silence is editorial, and reading it as content is the whole point of this project.

Synthesis

Tunneling sorts the four books by what they take a physics chapter to be. For Landau and Lifshitz it is an occasion to demonstrate exact technique, and they wring more solvable cases out of the barrier than most readers knew existed. For Born it is a phenomenon — alpha decay — whose explanation happens to require the barrier, and the barrier is in service of the nucleus, not the reverse. For Dirac it is simply not the kind of thing his book discusses. For Feynman it is a topic the tour route bypasses. The same effect anchors a calculation, explains a natural process, falls below the threshold of abstraction, and misses the itinerary — four times over.

The most illuminating pairing is Landau and Born, because between them they hold the two halves of how tunneling entered physics. Born supplies the 1928 moment when a baffling fact — nuclei emitting particles they could not classically release — found its explanation in a leaking wave. Landau supplies the mature technique that lets you compute the leak for any barrier you like, exactly where possible. Read together they show a discovery becoming a method: the crater that once seemed miraculous becomes one more potential to match wave functions across.

What no one disputes is the physics itself. A particle with too little energy gets through, with a probability that dies exponentially in the barrier's thickness, and the effect is not a mathematical artefact but the mechanism of radioactive decay and the reason certain electrons escape cold metals. The wave function's refusal to vanish at a wall — the same refusal that made the double slit interfere — is here doing work you can measure with a Geiger counter.
