Chapter IV · Part II — The New Mechanics

Quantum Behavior: The Double Slit and Amplitudes

Somewhere beneath every quantum calculation lies a single fact that no classical picture can absorb: when a particle can reach a place by more than one indistinguishable route, you do not add the probabilities of the routes — you add complex numbers, one per route, and square the sum. The cross-term that survives the squaring is interference. Everything else in quantum mechanics is, in a sense, bookkeeping around that one operation.

This chapter is where our four authors meet the operation head-on. Feynman builds his entire treatment on it, starting from the two-slit experiment and refusing to leave it. Dirac elevates it to an axiom — the principle of superposition — and derives the vector-space structure of quantum mechanics from it. Landau and Lifshitz state it as "the chief positive principle" and immediately draw the consequence that all the theory's equations must be linear. Born, who gave the amplitude its physical meaning, never writes the principle down as a principle at all.

The four treatments

Feynman: three rules and nothing else

Feynman's Volume III opens on the two-slit experiment and then, in the third chapter, distills the whole apparatus of quantum mechanics into three rules for combining amplitudes. First: the probability of an event is the absolute square of a complex amplitude. Second: when an outcome can be reached by several indistinguishable routes, the amplitudes for the routes add. Third: the amplitude for a route taken in stages is the product of the amplitudes for the stages.

What is radical is the claim that this is enough. Feynman calls the approach "a pedagogical experiment," and its wager is that the so-called advanced parts of quantum mechanics — spin, identical particles — are actually the simplest, once you give up trying to picture a particle threading space and work directly with amplitudes instead. He writes them in Dirac's bracket notation, $\langle x \mid s\rangle$ for "the amplitude that a particle from $s$ arrives at $x$," and shows that a two-stage journey factorizes: $\langle x \mid s\rangle_{\text{via }1} = \langle x \mid 1\rangle\langle 1 \mid s\rangle$.

The decisive move is his rule about when not to add amplitudes. If the final states differ in any way that could in principle be told apart — a scattered photon that marks which slit, a flipped nuclear spin that records which atom did the scattering — then you square first and add the probabilities. If no such record can exist even in principle, you add the amplitudes first. Feynman's slogan is that nature behaves this way whether or not you bother to look: "Nature does not know what you are looking at." The distinction is not about the observer's knowledge but about whether a distinguishing record exists anywhere in the world.

Dirac: superposition as the founding axiom

Dirac does not wait for an experiment to motivate superposition; he opens his book with it. His concrete anchor is the polarization of a photon: superpose two perpendicular plane-polarized states with equal weight and a phase, and you get circular, or diagonal, or elliptical polarization. From there he extracts the non-classical essence in a single sentence's worth of thought. Suppose an observation gives result $a$ with certainty on state $A$, and result $b$ with certainty on state $B$. On the superposition, the result is sometimes $a$ and sometimes $b$, never something in between. The intermediate character of the superposed state shows up in the probabilities, not in the outcomes.

This is where Dirac plants the flag that the others build on: superposition only makes sense because measurement is indeterminate. He then formalizes it. States correspond to vectors — his "ket vectors," written $|A\rangle$ — that can be added and scaled by complex numbers, so that a superposition is literally a linear combination $c_1|A\rangle + c_2|B\rangle$. One further assumption gives the theory its peculiar geometry: superposing a state with itself must return the same state, which forces $|A\rangle$ and any nonzero multiple of it to mean the same physical state. A quantum state is a direction in the vector space, not a vector of definite length — and the zero vector corresponds to no state at all.

Dirac is careful to warn against the analogy the mathematics invites. Because the equations are linear, one is tempted to compare quantum states with vibrating strings and membranes, which also superpose. He insists the resemblance is misleading: classical superposition of a state with itself gives a bigger oscillation, a genuinely different state, whereas quantum superposition of a state with itself gives nothing new. The linearity is the same; the meaning is not.

Landau & Lifshitz: linearity as a corollary

Landau and Lifshitz reach the same principle by a different door. They introduce the wave function $\psi(q)$ first, with $|\psi|^2\,dq$ as the probability of finding the coordinates in $dq$, and only then state superposition as a set of propositions about wave functions. Their formulation is operational: if a measurement yields result 1 with certainty in state $\psi_1$ and result 2 in $\psi_2$, then any linear combination $c_1\psi_1 + c_2\psi_2$ is a state in which the measurement yields either result 1 or result 2. They call this "the chief positive principle" of quantum mechanics — positive because the uncertainty principle of their opening section was, in their words, negative in content, and a theory cannot be built on prohibitions alone.

The corollary they draw immediately is the one that matters for everything after: because states superpose linearly, every equation that wave functions satisfy must itself be linear in $\psi$. That single sentence licenses the entire operator formalism to come — the Schrödinger equation, the eigenvalue problems, the expansions in complete sets. Where Feynman keeps the linearity implicit in his adding of amplitudes, and Dirac makes it geometry, Landau and Lifshitz name it as a constraint on the form of the laws and move on.

Born: the meaning without the principle

Born's place in this chapter is the strangest and, historically, the most consequential. Atomic Physics has no section called "the principle of superposition." What it has instead is the thing that makes superposition physics rather than mathematics: the interpretation of the squared wave amplitude as a probability. Born arrived at it in 1926 by comparing the scattering of electrons with the scattering of light, reasoning that the number of particles should be proportional to the intensity the wave theory computes. The quantity we now add and square, the amplitude, is meaningful because Born said what its square counts.

So Born supplies the semantics while declining to axiomatize the syntax. He is wary of the wave-packet reading that superposition tempts — a particle built from superposed wave trains, he notes, disperses like a ripple on water and cannot be the particle itself. His caution is the caution of someone who watched the interpretation get fixed in real time and knew how easily it could be misread. That he never elevates superposition to a stated principle is not an omission; it reflects a book organized around phenomena and their statistical reading rather than around a scheme of axioms.

Synthesis

Read together, the four treatments form a nearly complete logical circuit around a single idea, each author holding a different arc. Feynman has the operation — add amplitudes for indistinguishable routes — in its barest experimental form. Dirac has the axiom that turns the operation into a vector space and the insight that it only coheres because measurement is indeterminate. Landau and Lifshitz have the structural payoff, linearity of the laws. Born has the interpretation that lets any of it touch a laboratory.

The sharpest contrast is between Feynman and Dirac, and it is a contrast of direction rather than content. Feynman starts from the two-slit pattern and earns the amplitude formalism as its explanation; the abstraction is the destination. Dirac starts from the abstraction — states superpose — and treats polarization as an illustration of a principle already in hand; the experiment is a footnote. They use the same bracket notation, invented by Dirac and adopted by Feynman, to travel the same road in opposite directions.

What all four refuse, in their different registers, is the picture of a wave as a real thing in space. Feynman warns against attributing too much reality to the waves; Dirac warns that the vibrating-membrane analogy misleads; Landau and Lifshitz note that for two particles the amplitude lives in a six-dimensional configuration space, not ordinary space; Born watched the naive wave-packet electron dissolve and refused it. The amplitude is not a substance. It is a bookkeeping device of astonishing reach, and the double slit is the smallest experiment that forces you to keep its books correctly.
