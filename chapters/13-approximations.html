Chapter XIII · Part III — The Physics

Approximation Methods

Hydrogen can be solved exactly; almost nothing else can. Helium already has too many coordinates, and every real atom, molecule, and collision beyond the simplest is a problem the Schrödinger equation poses but cannot answer in closed form. Perturbation theory is the response: split the Hamiltonian into a part you can solve and a small remainder, and compute the corrections the remainder introduces as a series. It is less a single technique than a way of thinking — the habit of treating a hard problem as an easy one slightly disturbed — and the four authors reveal their characters in how they frame it.

This is a short chapter, because the mathematics is standard and the interest is in stance rather than result. Landau and Lifshitz build the general apparatus and turn its crank. Dirac steps back first to classify what perturbation theory even is, splitting it into two methods before computing anything. Born reaches for it as the practical tool that makes real spectra calculable. Feynman, characteristically, largely does without it — by confining himself to systems he can solve exactly.

The four treatments

Landau & Lifshitz: turning the crank

Landau and Lifshitz treat perturbation theory as a systematic apparatus to be built once and applied everywhere. They derive the first-order energy correction as the diagonal matrix element of the perturbation, the second-order correction as a sum over intermediate states with energy denominators, and — where the unperturbed level is degenerate — the corrections as the roots of a secular equation, a determinant whose vanishing fixes the splitting. They borrow the name, they note, from celestial mechanics, and the analogy is apt: a degenerate level, like a resonant orbit, splits when disturbed. They then extend the machinery to time-dependent perturbations, computing transition probabilities between states via the variation of the expansion coefficients, and specialize to periodic perturbations, where a resonance condition governs which transitions occur.

The treatment is thorough and impersonal, exactly as their book is everywhere. The degenerate case, the continuous spectrum, the periodic perturbation — each is handled as one more entry in a catalogue. What Landau and Lifshitz supply is the reference apparatus a working physicist reaches for, complete and general, with the understanding that in the majority of cases the first approximation already suffices.

Dirac: classifying before computing

Dirac, before he calculates anything, draws a distinction that organizes the whole subject. There are, he says, two distinct methods in perturbation theory. In the first, the perturbation causes a modification of the states of motion — one compares the stationary states of the perturbed system with those of the unperturbed system, and this is the method for energy-level shifts. In the second, the perturbation causes transitions — one takes an unperturbed stationary state and watches it evolve, changing into other states under the disturbance, and this is the method for anything involving time, including absorption, emission, and collisions.

Having drawn the distinction, he derives the energy corrections through his bracket algebra, recovering the first-order diagonal element and the second-order sum over states, and noting that the degenerate case corresponds to the "secular perturbations" of classical mechanics — the same borrowing Landau makes, from the other side. He credits the general recurrence formulas for the higher orders to Born, Heisenberg, and Jordan. Dirac's contribution here is less a calculation than a taxonomy: the recognition that "perturbation theory" names two different activities, and that knowing which one a problem calls for is the first step in solving it.

Born: the tool that makes atoms calculable

Born treats perturbation theory as the instrument that turns quantum mechanics from a theory of hydrogen into a theory of matter. He is candid that beyond the simplest systems exact solution is hopeless — helium already needs six coordinates, lithium nine — and that one must be content with approximation. But the approximation works: the lowest terms of many atomic configurations, he reports, have been computed to excellent agreement with experiment, beginning with Hylleraas's treatment of helium around 1930. His interest is in the results the method delivers — atomic spectra, the screening of the nucleus by inner electrons, and, at the frontier of his latest edition, the Lamb shift computed from renormalized quantum electrodynamics to a part in ten thousand.

Where Landau builds the apparatus and Dirac classifies it, Born points at what it buys. Perturbation theory, for him, is justified by the elements it makes calculable — it is the reason the periodic table and the atomic spectra of the previous chapters are not merely qualitative stories but quantitative predictions. His entry point is once again the evidence: the measured spectra that the method, pushed far enough, reproduces.

Feynman: solving exactly instead

Feynman's Volume III has no chapter that develops perturbation theory as a general apparatus, and the reason is a deliberate change of strategy. Rather than take hard problems and approximate them, he restricts his attention to systems simple enough to solve exactly — above all the two-state system, whose Hamiltonian is a $2\times 2$ matrix that can always be diagonalized in closed form. The ammonia molecule, with its nitrogen able to sit on either side of the hydrogen plane, is his exemplar: he writes the two-state Hamiltonian, solves it exactly for the split energy levels, and from that exact solution reads off real physics, up to the maser.

This is not a claim that Feynman never approximates; it is a claim about where he puts his weight. The others devote whole chapters to the systematic theory of the insoluble; Feynman spends his on a family of soluble systems rich enough to carry the ideas. The contrast is the same one that ran through the wavefunction chapter, where he reached the Schrödinger equation late and reluctantly: given the choice between the general machinery and the concrete exact case, he takes the case. His cell in the comparison is not empty but redirected — the approximation the others build, he arranges not to need.

Synthesis

Perturbation theory is where the four temperaments show in miniature, and the chapter is short because the differences are clean. Landau and Lifshitz build the general apparatus and apply it without comment. Dirac classifies the subject before touching a calculation, extracting the distinction between shifting states and causing transitions that the others use implicitly. Born measures the theory by the spectra it makes computable. Feynman sidesteps it, working with exactly solvable two-state systems rather than approximating hard ones. Apparatus, taxonomy, application, and avoidance: four relationships to the same indispensable method.

The revealing pairing is Landau and Feynman, and it recapitulates the axis of the whole project. Landau's instinct is to build the general tool, because a physicist will face problems no exact method can touch and needs a systematic way to approximate them. Feynman's instinct is to find the simplest system that still teaches the lesson, because an exact solution you understand is worth more than an approximate one you have merely computed. Neither is wrong; the working physicist needs Landau's apparatus, and the student learning to think needs Feynman's exactly-soluble cases. That perturbation theory can be presented either as the center of the subject or as a thing to be routed around is the clearest possible demonstration that a method is never separable from a temperament.
